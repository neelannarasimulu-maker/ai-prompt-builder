## LinkedIn Post Text

South African supply chains cannot plan around average lead times anymore

In South Africa, lead-time risk is not only a supplier problem. It is a ports, rail, road, water, power and coordination problem.

South Africa is moving through freight reform and port/rail recovery. That matters. But operators still face uneven reliability across routes, corridors, suppliers and service levels.

The executive issue is not only the average lead time. It is the range of possible outcomes. A shipment that usually takes 7 days is not a 7-day risk if it can become 11, 14 or 21 days when the route changes, a port delays, rail capacity shifts, road congestion increases, supplier capacity tightens, municipal services fail or energy reliability affects execution.

This is why average lead time can become a dangerous comfort metric. For South African operators, the better question is: how quickly can we detect that a supply promise is no longer true?

That requires visibility across supplier commitments, inbound stock, branch demand, current inventory, delivery status, exception ownership and decision timing.

South Africa's logistics reform agenda is important, but operators cannot wait for the macro system to become perfectly reliable before improving their own operating control.

SupplySync360 is designed to make these moving signals visible in one operating layer, so teams can respond before a delayed update becomes a stockout, service failure or emergency purchase.

Follow SupplySync360 for practical South African supply-chain operating insights.
