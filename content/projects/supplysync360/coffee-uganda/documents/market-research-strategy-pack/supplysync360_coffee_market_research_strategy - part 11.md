# Coffee Economics Framework

## Understanding Coffee Value Creation

Many new importers assume profitability is driven primarily by buying cheaply and selling expensively.

In reality, coffee profitability is influenced by multiple variables.

These include:

### Origin Cost

The cost paid to the supplier.

### Freight Cost

Transportation from origin to South Africa.

### Insurance Cost

Protection against transit risk.

### Customs and Clearance

Import administration costs.

### Warehousing

Storage and inventory handling.

### Distribution

Delivery to customers.

### Inventory Turnover

Speed at which stock converts into cash.

The interaction of these variables ultimately determines profitability.

# Working Capital Dynamics

## Why Working Capital Matters

Coffee import businesses consume capital before generating revenue.

This occurs because payment timing rarely aligns with inventory movement.

A typical sequence is:

Coffee Ordered

↓

Supplier Paid

↓

Coffee Shipped

↓

Coffee Arrives

↓

Coffee Sold

↓

Customer Pays

This creates a funding gap.

The larger the inventory position, the larger the working-capital requirement.

---

# Working Capital Risk

The greatest risk during early stages is not necessarily poor margins.

The greatest risk is insufficient working capital.

Many import businesses fail despite positive gross margins because inventory growth exceeds available cash resources.

# Pilot Economics

## Pilot Philosophy

The purpose of a pilot is validation.

The objective is not maximum profitability.

Pilot programmes should answer:

- Can suppliers perform?
- Can buyers be secured?
- Can logistics be managed?
- Can quality be maintained?

# Growth Economics

## 5 Ton Scenario

The business begins transitioning from pilot to commercial operation.

Advantages emerge from:

- Improved purchasing leverage
- Better freight economics
- Greater customer diversification

This is typically where meaningful commercial viability becomes visible.

## Strategic Considerations

Growth should remain controlled.

- Inventory risk

- Supplier dependency

Growth should follow demand rather than precede it.

# Gross Margin Thinking

## Common Mistake

Many importers focus exclusively on percentage margin.

This can be misleading.

A 30% margin on slow-moving inventory may be less attractive than a 15% margin on rapidly moving inventory.

---

## Recommended Approach

Evaluate:

Gross Margin

-

Inventory Turnover

-

Working-Capital Efficiency

rather than gross margin alone.

# Financial Recommendation

The economics support proceeding with a pilot programme.

However, the opportunity should be approached as a staged investment rather than a large-scale commitment.

Uganda offers the strongest economics for initial validation.

Ethiopia offers the strongest economics for premium expansion.

Together they create a diversified portfolio capable of supporting both commercial and specialty market segments.

The recommended approach is therefore:

Uganda First.

Ethiopia Second.

Scale Through Validated Demand.

Leverage SupplySync360 Technology As The Long-Term Differentiator.

This strategy provides the strongest balance between risk, capital efficiency, scalability and long-term value creation.
