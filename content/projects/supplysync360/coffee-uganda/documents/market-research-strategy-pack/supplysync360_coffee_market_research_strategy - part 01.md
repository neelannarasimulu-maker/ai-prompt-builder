# Document Purpose

This report has been prepared to assess the feasibility, attractiveness and strategic viability of importing coffee from East Africa, specifically Uganda and Ethiopia, into South Africa.

The report has been commissioned following discussions between SupplySync360 and Alimony Godfrey regarding the potential export of coffee into South Africa. Initial discussions focused on Uganda as a potential sourcing country. Subsequent correspondence indicated that sourcing options may include both Uganda and Ethiopia depending on commercial, operational and quality considerations.

The purpose of this report is therefore not merely to evaluate coffee as a trading opportunity, but to determine whether a sustainable and scalable coffee import business can be established and whether such an initiative aligns strategically with SupplySync360's longer-term supply-chain and trade-enablement ambitions.

This report has been structured as an executive-level strategic assessment rather than a simple market research document. The emphasis is on commercial viability, operational practicality, risk management, scalability and long-term strategic fit.

# Executive Summary

South Africa represents one of the most attractive coffee import markets in Sub-Saharan Africa.

Unlike many African markets that remain primarily tea-consuming economies, South Africa has developed a sophisticated coffee ecosystem comprising importers, roasters, café chains, hospitality providers, specialty coffee operators, retailers and corporate coffee-service providers.

Despite limited domestic coffee production, coffee consumption continues to grow across multiple customer segments. This growth is being driven by urbanisation, increasing consumer sophistication, rising demand for premium products and the continued expansion of café culture across major metropolitan areas.

The most important characteristic of the South African coffee market is its structural dependence on imports.

Coffee consumption substantially exceeds domestic production capacity, creating an ongoing requirement for imported coffee beans. Imported green coffee forms the backbone of the South African coffee value chain.

Approximately 22.7 million kilograms of non-decaffeinated green coffee were imported into South Africa during 2024. By comparison, approximately 1.4 million kilograms of roasted coffee were imported during the same period.

This distinction is critical.

The South African market does not primarily import finished coffee products. Instead, it imports green coffee beans and performs roasting, blending, packaging and brand development domestically.

For a prospective importer, this creates a clear market-entry pathway.

Rather than competing immediately in consumer retail markets, an importer can participate in the green coffee supply chain by supplying local roasters, coffee brands, hospitality providers and specialty operators.

Uganda already has a recognised presence within this market. Import statistics indicate that Uganda supplied approximately 1.23 million kilograms of green coffee to South Africa during 2024, placing it among the leading coffee origins serving the market.

This provides an important validation point.

The opportunity is not to create demand for Ugandan coffee. Demand already exists.

The opportunity is to increase participation within an established market that already purchases Ugandan coffee.

Ethiopia presents a different but complementary opportunity.

Where Uganda's strengths lie in scale, consistency, Robusta production and competitive economics, Ethiopia's strengths lie in specialty Arabica production, global recognition and premium positioning.

The strategic question is therefore not whether Uganda or Ethiopia can supply coffee to South Africa.

The strategic question is which sourcing strategy best aligns with SupplySync360's objectives, capabilities and risk appetite.

The preliminary conclusion of this report is that Uganda represents the strongest initial market-entry opportunity due to lower complexity, greater scalability and more favourable economics. Ethiopia should be viewed as a complementary Phase 2 opportunity capable of enhancing margins, differentiation and premium market positioning.

The broader strategic opportunity extends beyond coffee itself.

Coffee may represent the first practical implementation of a wider East Africa to South Africa trade corridor enabled by SupplySync360's supply-chain expertise, supplier coordination capabilities and technology platform.

# South African Coffee Market Analysis

## Overview of the South African Coffee Industry

South Africa possesses one of the most developed coffee markets on the African continent.

While historically tea dominated hot beverage consumption, coffee has experienced significant growth over the past two decades.

This growth has been supported by several structural factors.

Firstly, urbanisation has increased demand for convenient, premium and experience-driven food and beverage products.

Secondly, international café brands and local coffee operators have expanded consumer awareness of specialty coffee.

Thirdly, increased disposable income among middle-income consumers has supported demand for premium coffee experiences.

Finally, the growth of remote working, co-working environments and modern office culture has expanded demand for coffee beyond traditional retail channels.

The result is a multi-layered coffee ecosystem that incorporates commercial coffee, specialty coffee, premium coffee, foodservice coffee and private-label coffee products.

Unlike some emerging coffee markets, South Africa's coffee sector has matured sufficiently to support significant differentiation across quality levels and price points.

---

## Structure of the South African Coffee Value Chain

The South African coffee value chain can be divided into six primary segments.

### Green Coffee Importers

These businesses import green coffee beans from producing countries.

They may supply roasters directly or maintain warehousing operations that support local distribution.

### Coffee Roasters

Roasters purchase green coffee and transform it into finished products.

Roasters range from large industrial operations to highly specialised artisanal producers.

### Coffee Brands

Some organisations operate primarily as brands, outsourcing roasting while focusing on marketing, distribution and customer acquisition.

### Hospitality and Foodservice

Hotels, restaurants, cafés and catering businesses represent a major demand segment.

### Retail

Retail includes supermarkets, specialty food retailers and e-commerce channels.

### Corporate Coffee Services

This segment supplies offices, co-working spaces, conference venues and institutions.

Each segment possesses different buying behaviours, volume requirements and quality expectations.

Understanding these differences is critical when determining a market-entry strategy.

---

## Domestic Coffee Production

South Africa does produce coffee domestically.

However, production volumes remain relatively small compared to national consumption requirements.

Commercial coffee production occurs primarily in KwaZulu-Natal, Mpumalanga and selected regions with suitable climatic conditions.

Although locally produced coffee often commands premium pricing due to its novelty and limited availability, domestic production is insufficient to satisfy broader market demand.

Consequently, imports remain essential.

This structural imbalance between consumption and production creates the foundation for the import opportunity being evaluated within this report.

---

## Coffee Consumption Trends

Several consumption trends are shaping the future of the South African coffee market.

### Premiumisation

Consumers increasingly demonstrate a willingness to pay for higher-quality coffee products.

This trend supports demand for specialty coffee, single-origin offerings and traceable sourcing stories.

### Café Culture Expansion

Coffee shops continue to expand across major urban centres including Johannesburg, Cape Town, Durban and Pretoria.

Independent operators are increasingly competing through quality differentiation rather than convenience alone.

### Specialty Coffee Growth

Specialty coffee remains one of the fastest-growing segments within the market.

Consumers are becoming more knowledgeable regarding origin, processing methods and flavour profiles.

### Ethical and Sustainable Sourcing

Growing awareness of sustainability issues has increased interest in direct-trade relationships, producer stories and environmentally responsible sourcing practices.

### Convenience

At the same time, convenience remains important.

Capsules, pods, ready-to-drink coffee and workplace coffee solutions continue to grow.

These trends collectively create opportunities for both commercial-grade and premium-grade imported coffee.

# South African Coffee Import Market

## Import Dependency

The defining characteristic of the South African coffee industry is import dependency.

Coffee consumption substantially exceeds domestic production.

As a result, the industry relies heavily on imported coffee beans to satisfy demand.

Import data demonstrates that green coffee remains the dominant import category.

This reflects the preference of South African businesses to roast and blend coffee locally.

Local roasting allows operators to maintain quality control, customise blends and differentiate products according to customer preferences.

For potential importers, this creates a favourable market structure because green coffee can serve multiple downstream customer categories simultaneously.

---

## Major Coffee Origins

South Africa sources coffee from a diverse range of producing countries.

Key origins include:

- Brazil
- Ethiopia
- Tanzania
- Uganda
- Guatemala
- Colombia
- Vietnam
- Rwanda

Each origin occupies a different position within the market.

Brazil dominates commercial supply.

Ethiopia dominates premium African specialty positioning.

Uganda serves both commercial and specialty opportunities.

Tanzania provides a balance between quality and regional proximity.

Understanding these origin dynamics will become critical when comparing Uganda and Ethiopia in subsequent sections of this report.
