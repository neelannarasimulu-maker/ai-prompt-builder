Domestic coffee farming exists, mainly in KwaZulu-Natal, Mpumalanga and some smaller boutique areas, but local production is not large enough to satisfy national demand. ## 3. South Africa Coffee Market Overview

### 3.1 Market structure

South Africa’s coffee market can be divided into six major segments:

| Segment | Description | Relevance to Ugandan coffee |
|---|---|---|
| Green coffee imports | Raw green beans imported for roasting, blending and processing | Highest relevance for initial entry |
| Commercial roasters | Roasters serving cafés, restaurants, offices, hotels and private-label customers | High relevance |
| Specialty roasters | Independent and premium roasters focused on quality, origin and story | High relevance for Arabica and fine Robusta |
| Café chains | Branded coffee-led chains and restaurant groups | Medium to high relevance, but harder to access directly |
| Packaged retail | Instant, ground coffee, beans, capsules and private label | Attractive later-stage opportunity |
| Office, hospitality and foodservice | Coffee supplied with machines, service and replenishment | Strong opportunity for recurring B2B demand |

The market is not homogeneous. South Africa has both value-driven coffee demand and premium/specialty demand. A successful Ugandan coffee strategy should therefore avoid a single-product approach. It should offer both commercial-grade coffee and differentiated premium lots.

### 3.2 Domestic production is small

South Africa grows some coffee locally, but local production is small and cannot materially replace imports. Public agricultural guidance and ecosystem research describe South African coffee as a niche crop, with production concentrated in suitable subtropical regions. [R11, R12]

This matters because Uganda is not competing against a large domestic South African farming base. Uganda is competing mainly against imported origins such as Brazil, Vietnam, Ethiopia, Tanzania, Guatemala, Colombia and Rwanda.

### 3.3 Imports dominate the market

In 2024, South Africa imported **22.694 million kg** of non-decaffeinated green coffee valued at **US$98.4 million**. The largest origins were Brazil, Ethiopia, Tanzania, Uganda and Guatemala. [R1]

In the same year, South Africa imported **1.417 million kg** of roasted non-decaffeinated coffee valued at **US$20.54 million**, with Switzerland and Italy among the leading origins. [R2]

This shows that South Africa has two different coffee markets:

1. A **green-bean market** for local roasting and blending.
2. A **premium imported roasted/processed market** for branded, convenience and specialty products.

For Uganda, the first market is the priority.

## 6. Trade Agreements, Tariffs and Taxes

### 6.1 Trade agreement position

There does not appear to be a standalone South Africa-Uganda bilateral coffee trade agreement in the reviewed official sources. The relevant frameworks are:

- WTO membership by both South Africa and Uganda;
- AfCFTA as a continental trade framework;
- South Africa’s customs tariff regime administered by SARS;
- South Africa’s customs and VAT rules;
- any rules of origin or documentation requirements if preferential treatment is claimed.

South Africa is part of SACU, while Uganda is part of the East African Community and COMESA. However, for coffee entering South Africa, the key practical issue is not COMESA or EAC preference, because South Africa is not an EAC member and the South African customs treatment must be assessed under South African tariff lines and applicable trade agreements.

### 6.2 Tariffs on coffee

SARS Schedule 1 shows that green coffee is duty-free under the general tariff and also duty-free under AfCFTA.

| Product | HS code | General duty | AfCFTA duty | Practical implication |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|
| Green Arabica coffee | 0901.11.10 | Free | Free | No customs duty advantage needed |
| Green Robusta coffee | 0901.11.20 | Free | Free | Best initial route |
| Other green coffee | 0901.11.90 | Free | Free | Best initial route |
| Roasted non-decaf coffee | 0901.21 | 6c/kg | 2.4c/kg | Small AfCFTA saving possible |
| Roasted decaf coffee | 0901.22 | 6c/kg | 2.4c/kg | Small AfCFTA saving possible |
| Coffee husks and skins | 0901.90.10 | 20% | 8% | Less relevant |
| Coffee substitutes containing coffee | 0901.90.20 | 10c/kg | 4c/kg | Relevant only for formulated products |

Source: SARS Schedule 1, Chapter 9. [R4]

The key conclusion is that **AfCFTA does not create a meaningful duty saving for green Ugandan coffee because green coffee is already duty-free under the general tariff**. AfCFTA may matter more for future processed products, documentation, market access and regional trade positioning.

### 6.3 VAT treatment

SARS states that VAT on imported goods is 15%. For goods imported from outside the customs union, the VAT calculation uses the customs value plus a 10% uplift, plus any non-rebated duties, multiplied by 15%. [R5]

For green coffee, customs duty is zero, but import VAT still matters for cash flow.

Illustrative formula:

**Import VAT = [(Customs value + 10%) + non-rebated duties] × 15%**

For a VAT-registered South African importer, import VAT may be recoverable as input VAT, but it still creates a working-capital requirement. This is one of the reasons smaller businesses may prefer to buy from a local importer-distributor rather than import directly.

### 6.4 Importer registration and customs

SARS requires importers to register. Foreign importers may need to nominate a registered South African agent. Importers also need appropriate customs documentation and may use a customs clearing agent. [R6]

For SupplySync360, this means the business should decide whether to:

1. register as the South African importer of record;
2. partner with an existing importer/clearing agent; or
3. act initially as a commercial aggregator while a specialist importer handles customs.

The recommended path is to use a licensed clearing agent for the first transactions, while building internal import capability over time.

## 7. Regulatory and Product Compliance Considerations

South Africa regulates coffee, chicory and related products intended for sale in the Republic. The regulations cover product categories, standards, containers, labelling, origin references and traceability. [R9]

The regulations are especially important for roasted, ground, instant, capsule, concentrate and retail-ready products.

However, the regulations state that the sale restrictions do not apply to green coffee beans intended for further processing and/or roasting. [R9]

This supports the recommended market-entry strategy:

1. Start with green coffee imports.
2. Supply roasters and foodservice buyers.
3. Move into retail-ready products later, once product, labelling and compliance systems are ready.

For retail-ready products, attention will be needed on:

- product name/category;
- country of origin;
- packer/importer details;
- batch number or date code;
- claims such as “100% pure”, “single origin”, “espresso”, “organic” or “fair trade”;
- capsule/pod marking;
- ingredient declarations for blends, chicory mixtures, flavouring and premixes.

## 9. Why South African Companies Would Buy Ugandan Beans from SupplySync360

This is the most important strategic question. Buyers will not buy simply because the coffee is Ugandan. They will buy because SupplySync360 solves a problem for them.

### 9.1 They can access origin-direct supply without importing themselves

Many small and mid-sized roasters cannot efficiently import directly from Uganda. They may lack:

- container volumes;
- customs capability;
- supplier due diligence;
- working capital;
- origin relationships;
- freight management;
- quality assurance;
- storage;
- claims handling.

SupplySync360 can remove this burden by landing the coffee in South Africa and selling it in smaller lots.

### 9.2 Local stock reduces risk

A South African buyer is more likely to trial Ugandan coffee if the coffee is already landed, sampled, quality-checked and available locally.

Local landed stock reduces:

- lead-time uncertainty;
- customs delays;
- exchange-rate friction;
- minimum order burden;
- payment risk;
- quality-dispute risk;
- logistics complexity.

This is the buyer’s “easy yes” point.

### 9.3 Flexible lot sizes create a strong buying reason

A small roaster may want 60 kg, 120 kg, 300 kg or 1 tonne. They may not want a full container. A local importer-distributor can break bulk and sell in smaller commercial lots at a premium.

This is one of the clearest margin opportunities.

### 9.4 Better quality assurance

A serious buyer needs more than a WhatsApp price. They need:

- samples;
- cupping notes;
- moisture content;
- screen size;
- defect tolerance;
- grade;
- crop year;
- bag markings;
- origin details;
- lot traceability;
- claims process.

If SupplySync360 creates a disciplined quality-assurance layer, buyers will pay for reduced uncertainty.

### 9.5 Supply diversification

South Africa’s green coffee imports are heavily weighted toward Brazil, with meaningful contributions from Ethiopia, Tanzania, Uganda, Guatemala, Colombia, Vietnam and Rwanda. [R1]

Roasters may want alternative origins because of:

- price volatility;
- crop risk;
- supplier concentration;
- blend diversification;
- marketing differentiation;
- customer demand for African-origin coffees.

Ugandan coffee can be positioned as a diversification origin.

### 9.6 Uganda offers both Robusta and Arabica

Many origins are known mainly for one lane. Uganda can offer both:

- Robusta for body, crema, espresso blends and value;
- Arabica for specialty, single-origin and premium product lines.

This allows SupplySync360 to offer a portfolio rather than a single product.

### 9.7 Premium story with practical economics

Uganda has a strong African-origin story, but also a practical commercial base. This means the offer can appeal to both:

- premium roasters looking for traceable origin; and
- commercial roasters looking for blend economics.

That combination is valuable.

### 9.8 Technology-enabled procurement

SupplySync360’s core advantage is not coffee alone. It is operational coordination. A platform can show:

- available stock by grade;
- landed cost per lot;
- batch documentation;
- buyer order history;
- sample status;
- reorder alerts;
- warehouse location;
- delivery tracking;
- lot traceability;
- demand forecasts;
- buyer-specific price lists.

This turns a commodity trade into a managed supply network.

### 9.9 Buyers may prefer a South African counterparty

Some buyers may be hesitant to contract directly with a new exporter in Uganda. They may prefer a South African business that can invoice locally, receive local payment, manage VAT, and take responsibility for delivery.

This is especially important for smaller businesses and independent roasters.

### 9.10 The model can pass savings to smaller businesses

If SupplySync360 can aggregate demand and buy in bulk, smaller businesses can access better pricing than they would achieve individually. The model works if the importer captures enough margin while still making the landed price attractive.

The promise should not be “cheapest coffee”. The stronger promise is:

**Better access, better visibility, better consistency and better buying power.**

### 9.11 Buyers get a procurement partner, not only a supplier

Many roasters buy coffee as a product. SupplySync360 can offer coffee as a managed input. This means the buyer gets:

- product availability;
- documentation;
- ordering discipline;
- replacement options;
- consolidated invoices;
- local delivery;
- route planning;
- inventory planning;
- and repeatable procurement support.

This can be especially useful for businesses that are growing but do not yet have formal procurement teams.

### 9.12 SupplySync360 can support category innovation

Once green-bean demand is validated, SupplySync360 can support buyers with:

- seasonal Ugandan single-origin programs;
- co-branded origin releases;
- private-label coffee;
- office coffee packs;
- corporate gift packs;
- capsule pilots;
- hospitality blends;
- limited micro-lot drops.

This gives buyers innovation without forcing them to build sourcing capability from scratch.

## 11. High-Profit Coffee Opportunities

### 11.1 Specialty Robusta

Uganda has a natural advantage in Robusta. Specialty and fine Robusta can be positioned as a differentiated product, not just a cheap filler.

| Factor | Assessment |

| Factor | Assessment |
|---|---|

### 11.7 Coffee concentrate

Coffee concentrate can serve hotels, offices, caterers and quick-service formats.

| Factor | Assessment |

| Margin logic | Higher value-added format, but requires extraction and food-safety controls |

### 11.8 Corporate gifting

Premium Ugandan single-origin coffee can work well in corporate gifting.

| Factor | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Buyer logic | African-origin story, premium packaging, differentiated gifts |
| Margin logic | High margin per pack, especially for festive, conference and client gifting |
| Risk | Seasonal demand and packaging costs |
| Recommended approach | Use roasted Bugisu or Mt. Elgon limited-release packs for corporate gifting after securing a roasting and packaging partner |

### 11.9 Office coffee services

Office coffee is attractive because it creates recurring demand.

| Factor | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Buyer logic | Companies need machines, beans, replenishment, service and predictable monthly billing |
| Margin logic | Recurring revenue, but requires equipment/service capability |

## 13. Strategic Positioning

### 13.1 What SupplySync360 should not be

SupplySync360 should not position the business as:

- a casual broker;
- a one-off importer;
- a cheap-coffee reseller;
- a retail brand from day one;
- a “we can supply anything” commodity middleman.

That positioning is weak and easy to copy.

### 13.2 What SupplySync360 should be

The stronger positioning is:

**A South African importer-distributor and supply-chain platform for African-origin coffee, starting with Ugandan green beans and building toward value-added coffee products for roasters, cafés, hospitality and smaller businesses.**

This positions SupplySync360 as infrastructure, not just a seller.

### 13.3 Core value proposition

For roasters:

**Access Ugandan Robusta and Arabica locally, in flexible volumes, with transparent landed pricing and quality documentation.**

For cafés:

**Get differentiated African-origin coffee without managing import complexity.**

For smaller businesses:

**Benefit from bulk-origin buying power without having to import a full container.**

For Alimony Godfrey:

**Gain a South African market partner that can build buyer demand, manage imports and create repeatable distribution channels.**

## 15. Route-to-Market Strategy

### 15.1 Phase 1: Validation

Objective: Confirm product-market fit before importing at scale.

Actions:

1. Request samples from Alimony Godfrey.
2. Obtain grade sheets, prices, quantities and export documentation.
3. Select 5 to 10 South African roasters for sample testing.
4. Conduct blind and named cupping.
5. Capture buyer feedback.
6. Calculate landed cost for each grade.
7. Identify buyers willing to issue trial purchase orders.
8. Avoid full-container commitment until there is real buyer demand.

Success criteria:

- at least five serious buyer conversations;
- at least three cupping sessions;
- at least two letters of interest or trial orders;
- confirmed landed-cost model;
- clear grade priority.

### 15.2 Phase 2: Pilot import

Objective: Prove the import and distribution model.

Recommended pilot:

- one commercial Robusta lot;
- one premium Arabica or Washed Robusta lot;
- landed in South Africa;
- sold to a mix of roasters and smaller buyers.

Success criteria:

- shipment clears without major delay;
- quality matches pre-shipment samples;
- at least 70% of stock pre-sold or sold within 60 days;
- buyers provide repeat-order interest;
- gross margin meets minimum threshold.

### 15.3 Phase 3: Buyer aggregation platform

Objective: Turn the model into a repeatable supply-chain business.

Actions:

- list available lots online;
- allow buyer reservations;
- track inventory by grade and lot;
- create buyer-specific pricing;
- introduce recurring order schedules;
- collect demand forecasts;
- use technology to plan next imports.

Success criteria:

- 10 active B2B buyers;
- repeat orders from at least five buyers;
- container-scale demand forecast;
- positive gross margin after storage and finance;
- low claims rate.

### 15.4 Phase 4: Value-added products

Objective: Move up the margin ladder.

Potential products:

- roasted single-origin Uganda range;
- private-label office coffee;
- premium corporate gifting;
- capsules;
- hospitality packs;
- e-commerce specialty drops;
- limited-edition micro-lots.

Success criteria:

- validated green-coffee demand;
- reliable roasting partner;
- compliant packaging;
- defined sales channel;
- minimum margin above green-bean resale.

## 17. Other Considerations That Should Be Added to the Business Case

### 17.1 Quality claims and sample retention

Every shipment should have:

- pre-shipment sample;
- arrival sample;
- retained reference sample;
- moisture report;
- defect assessment;
- cup score or cupping notes;
- claims protocol.

Without this, quality disputes can become expensive and relationship-damaging.

### 17.2 Incoterms

The first deal must clearly define whether pricing is:

- EXW;
- FOB;
- CFR;
- CIF;
- DAP;
- or another Incoterm.

For early deals, CIF or CFR to Durban may be easier for buyer comparison, but SupplySync360 should obtain freight quotes before agreeing.

### 17.3 Payment terms

Coffee trade exposes both parties to risk. Possible structures include:

- advance payment for first samples;
- deposit on first shipment;
- balance against documents;
- letter of credit for larger shipments;
- escrow or trade-finance arrangement;
- local buyer pre-orders.

Do not provide long credit terms until buyer behaviour is proven.

### 17.4 Currency risk

The business will likely buy in USD and sell in ZAR. A weakening rand can destroy margin if pricing is not managed.

Mitigations:

- short quote validity;
- FX buffer;
- buyer deposits;
- ZAR price reset clauses;
- USD-linked pricing for large buyers.

### 17.5 Working capital

Import VAT, freight, port charges, storage and stockholding create cash pressure even if the business is profitable on paper.

The model needs a working-capital plan before the first shipment.

### 17.6 Storage risk

Green coffee must be stored correctly. Poor storage can cause:

- moisture damage;
- mould risk;
- bag deterioration;
- pest infestation;
- quality loss;
- claims.

Warehouse selection matters.

### 17.7 Insurance

Insurance should cover:

- marine cargo;
- theft;
- damage;
- port delays where possible;
- warehouse risks.

### 17.8 Phytosanitary and agricultural controls

Coffee is an agricultural product. Import requirements may include phytosanitary documentation and plant-health compliance. This must be confirmed with the clearing agent and relevant South African authorities before shipment.

### 17.9 Food safety and contaminants

Buyers may ask about:

- moisture;
- mould;
- pesticide use;
- ochratoxin risk;
- foreign matter;
- fumigation status;
- traceability;
- certifications.

The exporter should be ready with documentation.

### 17.10 Certification

Certification is not mandatory for all buyers, but it can unlock premium channels.

Possible certifications:

- organic;
- Fairtrade;
- Rainforest Alliance;
- UTZ or equivalent legacy programs;
- direct-trade documentation;
- women/youth farmer programs;
- sustainability traceability.

Do not claim certification unless valid documents exist.

### 17.11 Competition from existing importers

Existing importers and roasters may already have relationships with Brazil, Vietnam, Tanzania, Ethiopia and Uganda suppliers. SupplySync360 must avoid competing only on price.

Differentiation should come from:

- local break-bulk;
- flexible volumes;
- traceability;
- origin relationship;
- technology;
- quality control;
- buyer service;
- reliable replenishment.

### 17.12 Brand story

Ugandan coffee has a strong story, but the story must be specific.

Avoid vague statements such as “high-quality African coffee”.

Use specific origin language:

- Bugisu Arabica;
- Mt. Elgon;
- Rwenzori;
- Ugandan Robusta;
- washed process;
- natural process;
- screen size;
- farm/cooperative region;
- altitude where verified;
- flavour notes where cupped.

### 17.13 Data capture

Every buyer interaction should be captured:

- buyer type;
- current origins used;
- current monthly volume;
- preferred grades;
- target price;
- cupping feedback;
- objections;
- reorder potential.

This data becomes the strategic asset.

### 17.14 ESG and traceability expectations

Coffee buyers increasingly care about ethical sourcing, sustainability and traceability. SupplySync360 should prepare for:

- documented farm or cooperative origin;
- producer payment transparency where available;
- traceability from lot to buyer;
- claims discipline;
- environmental and social positioning;
- avoiding vague or unverifiable sustainability claims.

### 17.15 Route concentration risk

Uganda is landlocked, so logistics routes matter. Inland transport to an ocean port can affect timing, cost and reliability. SupplySync360 should compare:

- route options;
- transit times;
- freight forwarders;
- port congestion risk;
- insurance cover;
- customs documentation requirements;
- consolidation options.

### 17.16 Buyer education

Some buyers may not understand Uganda’s coffee profile beyond generic Robusta. SupplySync360 should create a short buyer education pack explaining:

- Uganda’s coffee regions;
- Robusta and Arabica distinction;
- key grades;
- cup profile;
- blend applications;
- specialty opportunities;
- sample evaluation process.

## 22. Final Recommendation

The opportunity is commercially promising, but it should be built carefully.

The first objective should not be to “sell coffee in South Africa”. The first objective should be to prove that specific Ugandan grades can win repeat orders from specific South African buyer segments at a landed cost that leaves margin for the importer and value for the buyer.

The recommended strategy is:

1. Validate demand with samples.
2. Secure buyer feedback and trial commitments.
3. Import a small, controlled pilot.
4. Break bulk and supply local buyers.
5. Use SupplySync360’s technology to manage stock, pricing, traceability and reorders.
6. Build toward value-added products only after repeat demand is proven.

The strongest business case for SupplySync360 is to become a trusted South African bridge between Ugandan origin supply and fragmented South African coffee demand.

The winning model is not just coffee trading.

It is **origin-backed, technology-enabled coffee supply-chain coordination**.
