The purpose of the 1 July meeting is to evaluate whether a practical coffee import opportunity exists between East African suppliers and South African coffee buyers.

The discussion should clarify whether the opportunity should begin with Uganda, Ethiopia or both countries. It should also confirm what coffee types are available, what quality and volumes can be supplied, whether samples can be provided, what export documentation exists and what commercial terms would be required for a pilot import.

# 3. South African Coffee Market Context

South Africa is a structurally import-dependent coffee market. Local coffee production exists, but it remains small relative to national consumption and commercial roasting demand.

The South African market has a developed coffee ecosystem comprising commercial roasters, specialty roasters, café chains, hospitality operators, office coffee suppliers, retail coffee brands and private-label manufacturers. This creates multiple potential buyer segments for imported coffee.

The most important market feature is that South Africa imports significantly more green coffee than roasted coffee. This indicates that local businesses prefer to import green beans and then roast, blend, package and distribute coffee locally.

This is important for SupplySync360 because it means the strongest initial opportunity is not to launch a finished retail coffee brand, but to import green coffee and supply South African coffee businesses that already understand roasting and distribution.

This supports a green-coffee-first market entry strategy.

# 6. Uganda Opportunity Summary

Uganda appears to be the strongest initial sourcing option for a South African pilot programme.

Uganda's strengths include:

- Large Robusta production.
- Growing Arabica production.
- Existing coffee exports into South Africa.
- Competitive indicative import value.
- Strong fit with commercial roasters and espresso blends.
- Better suitability for scalable import-distribution economics.

Uganda is likely to fit the following South African buyer segments:

| Buyer Segment | Fit With Uganda | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Commercial roasters | High | Need consistent green coffee and blend economics |
| Hospitality suppliers | High | Need reliable, cost-effective coffee supply |
| Corporate coffee programmes | High | Need recurring supply and predictable pricing |
| Espresso blend producers | High | Robusta can support body and crema |
| Specialty roasters | Medium | Selected Arabica or specialty Robusta lots may fit |

**Strategic view:**  
Uganda should be considered the preferred Phase 1 sourcing country if the objective is to validate imports, build buyer demand and develop a scalable importer-distributor model.

---

# 7. Ethiopia Opportunity Summary

Ethiopia appears to be the strongest premium and specialty expansion option.

Ethiopia's strengths include:

- Global reputation as a leading Arabica origin.
- Strong specialty coffee recognition.
- Higher implied import value into South Africa.
- Stronger fit with premium cafés and specialty roasters.
- Compelling origin story and traceability potential.

Ethiopia is likely to fit the following South African buyer segments:

| Buyer Segment | Fit With Ethiopia | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Specialty roasters | Very High | Demand for single-origin and premium Arabica |
| Premium cafés | High | Strong origin story and customer-facing value |
| Independent coffee brands | High | Useful for limited releases and differentiated products |
| Hospitality premium accounts | Medium | Fits high-end offerings |
| Commercial roasters | Medium to Low | Higher price may limit commercial blend use |

**Strategic view:**  
Ethiopia should be treated as a complementary premium origin rather than the first large-scale commercial entry option.

# 8. Recommended Sourcing Strategy

The recommended approach is a phased dual-origin strategy.

| Phase | Focus | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 | Uganda validation | Lower entry risk, commercial scale, existing South African demand |
| Phase 2 | Uganda pilot import | Validate logistics, buyer interest and repeat demand |
| Phase 3 | Ethiopia sample evaluation | Test premium and specialty demand |
| Phase 4 | Dual-origin portfolio | Combine Uganda volume with Ethiopia premium positioning |
| Phase 5 | Value-added expansion | Explore private-label, specialty lots, corporate gifting and premium products |

**Recommended pilot portfolio assumption:**  
A practical pilot could start with a Uganda-heavy portfolio and a smaller Ethiopia specialty component.

Illustrative split for discussion:

- 70% Uganda coffee for commercial and espresso-blend validation.
- 30% Ethiopia coffee for specialty and premium-buyer validation.

This split should only be confirmed after sample quality, pricing, supply capacity and buyer interest have been validated.

# 11. Key Risks To Manage

| Risk | Potential Impact | Control |
|---|---|---|
| Supplier reliability risk | Missed timelines or inconsistent delivery | Verify export history and supplier references |
| Quality risk | Buyer rejection or lower pricing | Use samples, cupping and retained reference samples |
| Logistics risk | Delays or cost overruns | Use experienced freight and clearing partners |
| Working-capital risk | Cash tied up in slow-moving stock | Pre-sell or validate buyer interest before shipment |
| Currency risk | Margin erosion | Use short quote validity and FX buffers |
| Buyer adoption risk | Weak demand after import | Conduct buyer testing before committing stock |
| Documentation risk | Import delays | Verify documents before shipment |
