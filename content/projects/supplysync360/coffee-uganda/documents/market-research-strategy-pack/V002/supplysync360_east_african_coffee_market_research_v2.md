# 1. Executive Research Summary

This document is a market research report, not a strategy or market-entry plan. Its purpose is to establish the factual basis for assessing whether coffee sourced from Uganda and Ethiopia could have viable demand in South Africa, which segments of the South African market are most relevant, which coffee product formats matter most, and what regulatory, logistics and commercial considerations should be understood before any strategy is finalised.

The research indicates that South Africa is a structurally import-dependent coffee market. Domestic coffee production exists but is limited and cannot satisfy the scale of national consumption, commercial roasting demand, hospitality demand, café demand and retail coffee demand. South Africa therefore relies on imported coffee, particularly green coffee beans, as the foundation of its coffee value chain.

The most important quantified finding is that South Africa imports far more green coffee than roasted coffee. In 2024, South Africa imported approximately 22.694 million kilograms of coffee that was not roasted and not decaffeinated under HS 090111. This category had an import value of approximately US$98.398 million. In comparison, South Africa imported approximately 1.417 million kilograms of roasted non-decaffeinated coffee under HS 090121, valued at approximately US$20.543 million. When decaffeinated green coffee and roasted decaffeinated coffee are included, the four main coffee import categories totalled approximately 24.551 million kilograms in 2024. Non-decaffeinated green coffee represented approximately 92% of that combined volume.

This supports a central market-research conclusion: the South African coffee import market is primarily a green coffee market. South African businesses generally import green beans and perform roasting, blending, packaging and brand development locally. This has significant implications for any future strategy, but this research document stops short of prescribing that strategy.

Uganda and Ethiopia are both already present in South African green coffee imports. In 2024, Ethiopia supplied approximately 1.387 million kilograms of non-decaffeinated green coffee to South Africa, valued at approximately US$8.298 million. Uganda supplied approximately 1.231 million kilograms, valued at approximately US$4.930 million. Ethiopia's implied import unit value was approximately US$5.98 per kilogram, while Uganda's implied import unit value was approximately US$4.01 per kilogram. These figures suggest that Ethiopia is positioned at a higher-value import level, consistent with its premium Arabica and specialty coffee reputation, while Uganda appears more strongly aligned with commercial-scale value, Robusta supply and broader blend economics.

The research also indicates that the South African market has multiple demand segments, including commercial roasters, specialty roasters, café chains, hospitality groups, office coffee providers, retail brands, private-label manufacturers and e-commerce coffee brands. These segments do not all require the same coffee. Commercial roasters and office coffee providers may prioritise consistency, price and supply reliability. Specialty roasters and premium cafés may prioritise origin, cup profile, traceability and uniqueness. Hospitality buyers may need a balance of quality, reliability and service. Private-label manufacturers may require consistency, regulatory compliance and repeatable supply.

Uganda and Ethiopia therefore appear to serve different but potentially complementary demand zones. Uganda's research profile is strongest for commercial Robusta, espresso blends, hospitality coffee, corporate coffee and selected Arabica or specialty Robusta opportunities. Ethiopia's research profile is strongest for specialty Arabica, premium cafés, single-origin programmes, direct-trade narratives, high-end hospitality and premium e-commerce products.

South African customs treatment is also relevant. The current SARS tariff schedule shows green coffee under HS 0901.11 and decaffeinated green coffee under HS 0901.12 as free of customs duty across general and preference columns. Roasted coffee under HS 0901.21 and 0901.22 carries a small specific duty under the general column, with different preferential treatments depending on trade arrangement. This strengthens the research finding that green coffee is structurally more attractive as an import category, although a future strategy document should test the total landed cost and commercial margin impact.

South African product regulations also matter. The South African coffee, chicory and related product regulations define green coffee beans and regulate categories such as roasted coffee beans, ground coffee, instant coffee, decaffeinated coffee, mixed coffee and related products intended for sale. Importantly, the restrictions on sale do not apply to green coffee beans intended for further processing and/or roasting purposes. This does not eliminate the need for documentation, customs compliance, food safety discipline or quality assurance, but it does distinguish green coffee from retail-ready packaged coffee products.

The research conclusion is that a real South African market exists, Uganda and Ethiopia both already participate in that market, green coffee is the dominant import format, and the South African buyer landscape is diverse enough to support multiple coffee-origin positions. The next document should be a separate Strategy and Market Entry Report that translates these findings into a SupplySync360 commercial position, operating model, pilot programme, financial model and execution roadmap.

This expanded Version 2.0 also analyses the full coffee value chain from nursery and farm production through harvesting, processing, milling, grading, export, inland logistics, ocean freight, customs clearance, warehousing, roasting, packaging, distribution and final sale in South Africa. It also identifies the research questions required to assess financial viability, sustainability, risk exposure and the practicality of long-term import arrangements. These additions are designed to make the document a more complete industry research base before any strategy or market-entry positioning is written.

# 4. Global Coffee Context

Coffee is one of the world's most traded agricultural commodities and one of the most widely consumed beverages globally. The global coffee market is shaped by two primary species: Arabica and Robusta. Arabica is generally associated with higher-altitude production, greater acidity, aromatic complexity and specialty coffee positioning. Robusta is generally associated with lower-altitude production, higher caffeine content, stronger body, stronger crema and commercial blend applications.

Global coffee supply has been affected by climate shocks, changing weather patterns, high prices and demand growth. Reuters reported in 2025 that the International Coffee Organization viewed global supply relief as possible only over a multi-year period as new plantations begin producing, while noting that successive deficits and climate pressures had pushed prices higher. This matters because import-dependent markets such as South Africa are exposed to global price cycles, freight costs, currency movements and origin-specific production conditions.

Within this environment, African coffee origins remain important because they provide both volume and differentiation. Ethiopia is one of the world's most recognised Arabica origins. Uganda is one of Africa's leading coffee exporters and is particularly important in Robusta. Tanzania, Rwanda, Burundi and Kenya also feature in South African green coffee import flows.

For South Africa, global coffee conditions matter because the country is a buyer rather than a major producer. A local importer or distributor is exposed to international supply, currency, freight and commodity price movements. At the same time, those global market pressures can create opportunities for alternative origins, diversified sourcing and stronger supply-chain coordination.

# 5. African Coffee Industry Overview

Africa occupies a distinctive position in the global coffee industry. The continent includes some of the world's most historically important and quality-recognised origins, but also a number of emerging commercial supply sources.

Ethiopia is the flagship African Arabica origin. Its coffee is associated with Yirgacheffe, Sidamo, Guji, Limu, Harrar and other recognised production areas. Ethiopia's coffees often command premium positioning because of origin recognition, cup profile and specialty demand.

Uganda is a major African exporter with a strong Robusta base. Reuters described Uganda as Africa's largest coffee exporter, followed by Ethiopia, and noted that Uganda primarily cultivates Robusta. Uganda's February 2025 exports were reported at 555,756 60-kilogram bags, with earnings of US$167.7 million for that month, and export earnings of US$1.7 billion over the twelve months to February 2025.

Tanzania, Kenya, Rwanda and Burundi also influence regional supply. These origins often matter in specialty coffee and regional sourcing discussions, but the present research focuses on Uganda and Ethiopia because Alimony Godfrey's communication identified those two supply options.

From a South African perspective, African coffee has two key research advantages. First, African origins are already present in the import data. Second, African coffee can support origin storytelling, traceability, sustainability narratives and regional trade positioning. These factors are not sufficient by themselves to prove a business case, but they create potential differentiation compared with purely commodity-origin imports.

# 6. South African Coffee Market Context

South Africa is a coffee-consuming market with limited domestic production. The country has an established coffee ecosystem that includes roasters, coffee brands, café operators, hospitality groups, office coffee suppliers, retailers, private-label manufacturers and coffee equipment/service providers.

The market's structural characteristic is import dependence. Unlike major producing countries such as Brazil, Vietnam, Ethiopia or Uganda, South Africa does not produce enough coffee domestically to satisfy commercial demand. The market therefore imports coffee as the foundation of its value chain.

Coffee consumption in South Africa spans several layers:

- In-home consumption through instant, ground, capsule and bean products.
- Coffee-shop and café consumption.
- Restaurant and hospitality consumption.
- Office and workplace coffee.
- Retail private-label and branded coffee.
- Specialty coffee and direct-to-consumer e-commerce.
- Corporate gifting and premium packs.

The strongest research signal is that South Africa imports green coffee in far greater volumes than roasted coffee. This suggests that local value addition through roasting, blending and packaging is a major feature of the market.

# 18. Regulatory Environment: South African Imports

Importing coffee into South Africa requires attention to customs registration, tariff classification, VAT, documentation, and product standards.

SARS notes that any local or foreign person who imports goods into South Africa must register as an importer. A foreign importer must register and nominate a registered agent located in South Africa before being registered to import goods.

SARS also explains that VAT on imported goods is calculated at 15% on the added tax value. The formula includes customs value plus a 10% uplift for goods imported from outside the Customs Union, plus any non-rebated duties, multiplied by 15%.

Coffee tariff classification matters because green coffee and roasted coffee are treated differently. The SARS tariff schedule lists HS 0901.11 coffee not roasted and not decaffeinated, including Arabica, Robusta and other, as free of duty. Decaffeinated green coffee under HS 0901.12 is also shown as free of duty. Roasted coffee under HS 0901.21 and 0901.22 carries a small specific duty under the general column.

South African coffee product regulations apply to coffee, chicory and related products intended for sale in South Africa. The regulations define green coffee beans as dried seeds of Coffea separated from non-food tissues. They also provide categories and standards for roasted coffee beans, ground coffee, instant coffee, decaffeinated coffee and other related products. Importantly, restrictions on sale do not apply to green coffee beans intended for further processing and/or roasting purposes.

For market research, this means green coffee is not only the dominant import category but also operationally distinct from retail-ready coffee products. Retail-ready products require stronger attention to labelling, standards, packaging and consumer-facing compliance.

# 19. Logistics And Shipping Considerations

Uganda and Ethiopia are both land-linked coffee origins relative to South African sea freight routes. Logistics must be understood country by country.

## 19.1 Uganda

Uganda is landlocked. Coffee typically moves by inland transport to export ports such as Mombasa in Kenya or Dar es Salaam in Tanzania. This creates additional transport stages before ocean freight.

Research considerations include:

- Inland transport cost from supplier or warehouse to port.
- Export documentation and handling.
- Port selection.
- Ocean freight to South Africa.
- Marine insurance.
- South African port handling.
- Customs clearing.
- Warehousing and local distribution.

The landlocked nature of Uganda does not make exports impractical, but it does increase operational coordination requirements.

## 19.2 Ethiopia

Ethiopian coffee exports typically move through Djibouti. Ethiopia's route to Djibouti is well-established and handles substantial trade volumes.

Research considerations include:

- Origin region to exporter or dry mill.
- Movement to Djibouti.
- Export documentation.
- Ocean freight to South Africa.
- Marine insurance.
- South African port and customs processes.
- Warehousing and local distribution.

Ethiopia's route may be clearer in port terms, but the coffee supply chain itself may be more complex because of origin specificity, smallholder aggregation, specialty traceability and export structures.

## 19.3 LCL Versus FCL

For pilot shipments, less-than-container-load or couriered samples may be appropriate. For commercial scale, full-container economics may become more attractive. However, full-container shipments create larger working-capital and inventory exposure.

A future strategy document should model:

- Sample courier stage.
- Small pilot shipment.
- 1-ton validation shipment.
- 5-ton growth shipment.
- 10-ton to 20-ton commercial shipment.
- LCL versus FCL cost sensitivity.
- Warehouse holding periods.
- Buyer payment cycles.

# 21. Certifications, Traceability And ESG

Traceability and sustainability are increasingly important in coffee markets. They do not replace price or quality, but they can influence buyer decisions, especially in specialty and premium segments.

For Ethiopia, traceability is particularly important because specialty buyers may require origin-region specificity. Ethiopian coffee's value is often tied to regions such as Yirgacheffe, Sidamo, Guji, Limu and Harrar. A supplier that cannot verify region, processing station or lot identity may struggle to realise premium value.

For Uganda, traceability is relevant in two ways. First, it can help differentiate selected Arabica and specialty Robusta lots. Second, it can support commercial buyers that increasingly need ESG-aligned sourcing and supply assurance.

Certifications may include Organic, Fairtrade, Rainforest Alliance or other recognised schemes. However, certifications should not be assumed. They must be verified with documentation, scope, validity period and certifying body.

ESG considerations include:

- Farmer income and payment structure.
- Child labour and labour standards.
- Environmental practices.
- Water use in washing stations.
- Deforestation risk.
- Farm traceability.
- Climate adaptation.
- Gender inclusion where relevant.
- Community development.

The market research conclusion is that ESG and traceability can add value, but only if evidence exists. Unsupported sustainability claims create reputational and commercial risk.

# 22. Trade Agreements And Tariff Context

The research did not identify a simple bilateral South Africa-Uganda or South Africa-Ethiopia coffee agreement that by itself creates a special coffee import advantage. The relevant trade context includes South Africa's tariff schedule, WTO frameworks, AfCFTA considerations and origin-specific documentation.

The most important practical point is tariff classification. Green coffee under HS 0901.11 and decaffeinated green coffee under HS 0901.12 is listed as free of customs duty in the SARS schedule. Roasted coffee carries a small specific duty under the general column, with preferential rates varying by trade arrangement.

AfCFTA may become strategically important for intra-African trade development, but for green coffee the existing free-duty position already reduces the tariff barrier. The bigger practical considerations are documentation, VAT cash flow, shipping, quality, supplier reliability and buyer demand.

A future strategy document should still confirm:

- Exact HS classification.
- Origin documentation.
- Certificate of origin requirements.
- Incoterms.
- VAT treatment.
- Whether any permits or phytosanitary documents apply to the specific shipment.
- Whether products are green beans for roasting or packaged retail products.

# 28. Key Market Research Findings

The following are the most important findings from the research.

## Finding 1: South Africa is import-dependent.

South Africa's domestic coffee production is limited relative to demand. Imports are essential to the coffee ecosystem.

## Finding 2: Green coffee dominates imports.

Non-decaffeinated green coffee represented approximately 92% of the combined volume of the four main coffee import categories in 2024.

## Finding 3: Uganda and Ethiopia already participate in South African imports.

Uganda supplied approximately 1.231 million kg and Ethiopia supplied approximately 1.387 million kg of non-decaffeinated green coffee to South Africa in 2024.

## Finding 4: Ethiopia carries a higher implied unit value.

Ethiopia's implied 2024 unit value was approximately US$5.98/kg, compared with Uganda's approximately US$4.01/kg.

## Finding 5: Uganda and Ethiopia serve different demand logic.

Uganda is more aligned with commercial scale, Robusta and blend applications. Ethiopia is more aligned with specialty Arabica, premium cafés and origin-led products.

## Finding 6: South African buyers are segmented.

Commercial roasters, specialty roasters, hospitality groups, office coffee providers and private-label manufacturers have different requirements.

## Finding 7: Import duties on green coffee are favourable.

The SARS tariff schedule lists green coffee lines under HS 0901.11 and 0901.12 as free of customs duty.

## Finding 8: VAT cash flow still matters.

SARS import VAT calculation can create working-capital pressure even where customs duty is free.

## Finding 9: Green coffee for further roasting is treated differently from retail-ready products.

South African coffee product regulations state that restrictions do not apply to green coffee beans intended for further processing and/or roasting purposes.

## Finding 10: Supplier due diligence is essential.

The market opportunity depends on verified suppliers, reliable quality, correct documentation, logistics execution and buyer validation.

# 30. Research Limitations

This research uses publicly available data and sources. It does not include proprietary importer databases, confidential buyer purchase records, paid industry databases, live freight quotes, supplier-specific documentation or verified buyer interviews.

WITS / UN Comtrade data is valuable for understanding trade flows but does not identify named South African importers, buyer contracts, quality grades, specialty status, landed cost or margins.

Implied unit values are calculated from aggregate import value and quantity. They should not be treated as supplier prices or commercial quotes.

Supplier capability cannot be confirmed from market research alone. It requires due diligence, samples, documentation, references and export verification.

Buyer demand cannot be confirmed from market research alone. It requires direct engagement with South African roasters, hospitality buyers, office coffee providers, coffee brands and other relevant channels.

This report should therefore be used as the research foundation for a separate Strategy and Market Entry Report.

# 31. End-To-End Coffee Value Chain: From Farm Origination To South African Sale

A complete coffee import opportunity cannot be assessed only at the point of export or import. Coffee quality, price, reliability and sustainability are shaped across the full value chain, beginning long before a shipment reaches a port. This section maps the practical stages from farm origination through import and final sale in South Africa.

## 31.1 Value Chain Overview

| Stage | Value Chain Step | Main Activities | Why It Matters For Import Research |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | Nursery and seedling development | Seed selection, nursery propagation, varietal selection, disease-resistant planting material | Determines long-term quality, yield potential and resilience |
| 2 | Farm establishment | Planting, spacing, shade management, soil preparation, water access | Influences productivity, sustainability and future supply capacity |
| 3 | Farm management | Pruning, fertilisation, pest control, disease management, weed control, shade-tree care | Affects yield, bean development, farm economics and quality consistency |
| 4 | Flowering and cherry development | Seasonal flowering, fruit set, maturation, rainfall sensitivity | Determines harvest timing and crop predictability |
| 5 | Harvesting | Selective hand picking or strip picking, collection of ripe cherries | Influences defect levels and cup quality |

| 7 | Drying | Raised beds, patios, mechanical dryers, moisture control | Critical for preventing mould, quality loss and shipment rejection |
| 8 | Hulling and milling | Removal of parchment or dried husk, cleaning, polishing where applicable | Converts parchment or dried cherry to exportable green coffee |
| 9 | Sorting and grading | Screen size, density sorting, hand sorting, defect removal, colour sorting | Determines grade, price band and buyer suitability |
| 10 | Quality control and cupping | Moisture testing, defect count, sample roasting, sensory evaluation | Confirms whether coffee meets commercial or specialty standards |

## 31.2 Research Implication

The value chain shows that an import opportunity is not created only by finding coffee at origin. Viability depends on the reliability of every link between production and sale. A supplier with attractive pricing but weak quality control, poor drying, unreliable documentation or uncertain logistics may create more risk than value. Conversely, a slightly higher-cost supplier with strong traceability, consistent processing and reliable export execution may be more commercially viable.

For market research purposes, each value-chain stage should be treated as a diligence point. Future strategy should only be developed once the most material value-chain risks have been evaluated.

# 32. Farm-Level Origination And Production Considerations

Coffee origination starts at farm level. The quality and sustainability of an import programme are shaped by how coffee is grown, harvested and handled before it becomes exportable green coffee.

## 32.1 Nursery And Planting Material

Nursery and seedling quality determines the long-term production base. Strong seedlings, appropriate varieties and disease-resistant planting material contribute to better farm productivity and supply continuity. Weak seedlings or poorly matched varieties can result in low yields, higher disease exposure and inconsistent coffee supply.

Research questions include:

| Research Question | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Which varieties are grown? | Determines flavour, productivity and disease tolerance |
| Are seedlings sourced from approved nurseries? | Reduces genetic and disease risk |
| Are varieties suitable for the local altitude and climate? | Affects long-term yield and quality |
| Is replanting occurring? | Indicates future supply resilience |

## 32.2 Farm Management

Farm management affects bean quality, crop reliability and sustainability. Key areas include shade management, soil fertility, pruning, pest control, disease control and water management.

Poor farm management can create yield volatility and quality variability. Strong farm management supports predictable production, better cherry development and greater consistency for buyers.

## 32.3 Harvesting Practices

Harvesting quality is one of the most important upstream determinants of final cup quality. Selective picking of ripe cherries normally produces better quality than indiscriminate stripping, but selective picking may be more labour-intensive and expensive.

Research questions include:

| Research Question | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Are cherries selectively picked or strip harvested? | Impacts defect rates and cup quality |
| How are under-ripe and over-ripe cherries handled? | Affects consistency and buyer acceptance |
| Are farmers paid incentives for quality? | Influences harvest discipline |
| Is harvesting labour available and reliable? | Affects crop collection and timing |

## 32.4 Farmer Economics

Long-term import sustainability depends on farmer economics. If farmers do not receive viable prices, supply may become unreliable, quality may decline, or farmers may shift to other crops. A sustainable import arrangement must consider whether farmers, cooperatives, processors, exporters and importers all have a workable economic position.

Market research should therefore evaluate not only buyer price, but also whether the supply chain supports recurring production and fair participation.

# 33. Processing, Milling, Grading And Export Preparation

After harvesting, coffee must be processed and prepared for export. This is where many quality risks either arise or are controlled.

## 33.1 Processing Methods

| Processing Method | Description | Market Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Natural / dry process | Cherries are dried with fruit still around the bean | Can create fruit-forward flavours; requires careful drying control |
| Washed / wet process | Fruit is removed before fermentation and washing | Often produces cleaner cup profile; requires water and processing infrastructure |
| Honey / pulped natural | Some mucilage remains during drying | Can balance sweetness and clarity; process control is important |
| Semi-washed | Partial washing or hybrid processing | May fit certain commercial profiles depending on quality control |

Processing method influences flavour, defect risk, buyer segment and price. Specialty buyers will often require detailed processing information. Commercial buyers may focus more on consistency, moisture, defects and blend performance.

## 33.2 Drying And Moisture Control

Drying is a critical control point. Coffee that is dried too quickly, too slowly, unevenly or under poor hygiene conditions may develop quality defects, mould risk or storage instability.

Research should request:

- Target moisture range.
- Drying method.
- Average drying time.
- Whether raised beds, patios or mechanical dryers are used.
- How moisture is measured.
- Whether lots are protected from rain and contamination.

## 33.3 Milling And Hulling

Milling converts parchment or dried cherry into exportable green coffee. Poor milling can increase broken beans, reduce grade, increase defects and damage appearance. For South African buyers, milled green coffee should be clean, properly sorted and packed for export.

## 33.4 Sorting And Grading

Sorting and grading determine the commercial class of the coffee. Common grading considerations include:

- Screen size.
- Defect count.
- Bean density.
- Colour.
- Moisture.
- Foreign matter.
- Cup quality.
- Origin region.
- Processing method.

A supplier should be able to explain the grading system used and provide grade sheets for each sample lot.

## 33.5 Export Lot Formation

Export lots should be identifiable. Lot identity matters for quality control, traceability, claims, repeat purchase and buyer confidence.

Minimum research requirements include:

| Requirement | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Lot code | Links sample to shipment |
| Crop year | Confirms freshness |
| Origin region | Supports buyer positioning |
| Grade | Supports pricing and quality expectation |
| Processing method | Supports cup expectation |
| Bag marks | Supports warehouse and shipping traceability |
| Retained sample | Supports claims management |

# 37. Financial Viability Research Framework

Financial viability should be assessed through a structured research framework rather than a single margin estimate. Coffee import economics are affected by origin price, quality, freight, VAT cash flow, storage duration, buyer pricing and stock turnover.

## 37.1 Financial Viability Questions

| Area | Research Question | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| Supplier price | What is the FOB or CIF price by coffee type and grade? | Determines base cost |
| Shipment size | What volumes are required for cost-efficient shipping? | Affects freight per kg |
| Logistics cost | What is the full origin-to-SA logistics cost? | Determines landed cost |
| VAT cash flow | How much cash is tied up at import? | Affects working capital |
| Warehousing | How long will stock be held? | Affects margin and quality |
| Buyer price | What price will South African buyers pay? | Determines revenue potential |
| Payment terms | When do customers pay? | Affects cash conversion cycle |
| Shrinkage/claims | What defect, loss or rejection risk exists? | Affects true margin |
| FX exposure | What USD/ZAR movement is possible? | Affects landed cost |
| Repeat demand | Will buyers reorder? | Determines sustainability |

## 37.2 Core Unit Economics Formula

For market research purposes, landed cost per kilogram should be analysed as:

**Landed cost per kg = supplier coffee cost + origin handling + inland freight + export documentation + ocean freight + insurance + South African port charges + clearing + warehousing + local distribution + finance cost**

Import VAT should be analysed as a cash-flow item. It may be recoverable for a VAT-registered importer, but it can still create funding pressure.

## 37.3 Financial Viability Indicators

| Indicator | Positive Signal | Warning Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Gross margin | Margin remains positive after all landed costs | Margin only works before logistics or VAT cash flow |
| Buyer demand | Buyers confirm interest after cupping | Interest is theoretical only |
| Stock turnover | Coffee can be sold within planned period | Coffee sits in warehouse too long |
| Repeat orders | Buyers request second orders | Buyers only test samples once |
| Supplier consistency | Shipment matches sample quality | Shipment differs from approved sample |
| FX protection | Quotes and payment windows are controlled | Long exposure to USD/ZAR movement |
| Shipment scale | Freight improves at larger volumes | Larger volumes create unsold inventory risk |

## 37.4 Example Viability Thresholds For Research

The following are not final strategy decisions. They are research thresholds that can guide future modelling.

| Metric | Research Threshold To Test |
|---|---|
| Sample approval | At least 5 South African buyer evaluations |
| Pilot buyer interest | At least 2 to 3 serious buyers before shipment |
| Pre-commitment | 50% to 70% of first shipment ideally allocated before import |
| Gross margin | Margin must remain positive after realistic landed cost |
| Stock turnover | Pilot stock should clear within 60 to 120 days |
| Quality claim rate | Low or manageable through supplier warranty/claims process |
| Supplier repeatability | Supplier can support repeat lots of similar quality |

# 38. Sustainability And Long-Term Viability Research

A coffee import programme can be commercially attractive in the short term but fragile in the long term if sustainability, farmer economics, climate exposure and supply resilience are weak. Long-term research should therefore assess economic, environmental, social and operational sustainability.

## 38.1 Economic Sustainability

Economic sustainability means that all participants in the value chain can continue participating over time.

| Participant | Sustainability Question |
|---|---|
| Farmers | Are farmgate prices viable enough to maintain production and quality? |
| Cooperatives / aggregators | Can they cover collection, processing and quality-control costs? |
| Exporters | Can they deliver documentation, logistics and working capital? |
| Importer | Can landed cost support buyer price and margin? |
| Roasters / buyers | Can they sell finished coffee profitably to their customers? |

If any part of the chain is structurally underpaid or overexposed, supply may deteriorate.

## 38.2 Environmental Sustainability

Environmental sustainability issues include:

- Shade-tree preservation.
- Soil health.
- Water use in washed processing.
- Wastewater handling.
- Climate adaptation.
- Deforestation risk.
- Biodiversity protection.
- Chemical use and pest control.

Climate change is a material long-term risk for coffee production globally. Temperature increases, erratic rainfall, drought, pests and disease can affect both yield and quality. Robusta is generally more heat-tolerant than Arabica, but it is not immune to climate stress.

## 38.3 Social Sustainability

Social sustainability issues include:

- Farmer payment transparency.
- Labour practices.
- Child labour prevention.
- Gender inclusion.
- Cooperative governance.
- Community benefit.
- Health and safety in processing.
- Fair treatment of seasonal workers.

These issues matter because coffee buyers, especially specialty and premium buyers, increasingly ask for credible proof of origin practices.

## 38.4 Supply Resilience

Long-term supply resilience depends on:

- Multiple farmers or supply clusters.
- Multiple processing stations.
- Repeatable quality control.
- Crop diversification.
- Climate adaptation.
- Strong local logistics.
- Exporter working capital.
- Documentation discipline.

Market research should therefore assess not only whether a supplier has coffee today, but whether the supplier can provide similar quality repeatedly over multiple seasons.

# 39. Long-Term Import Arrangement Models

Long-term import arrangements can take several forms. The best structure depends on supplier maturity, buyer demand, quality consistency, funding capacity and risk appetite.

## 39.1 Arrangement Types

| Arrangement Type | Description | Strengths | Risks |
|---|---|---|---|
| Spot purchase | Coffee bought lot-by-lot | Flexible and low commitment | No guaranteed supply or price stability |
| Sample-led purchase | Buyer approves samples before each purchase | Strong quality control | Slower process and limited scalability |
| Seasonal framework agreement | Parties agree expected volumes, pricing basis and quality specs for a season | Better planning and relationship stability | Requires supplier reliability and buyer demand |
| Forward contract | Price and volume agreed in advance | Supports planning | Exposes parties to price movement and quality risk |
| Offtake agreement | Buyer/importer commits to purchase agreed volumes | Supports supplier confidence | Risk if downstream demand is weak |
| Consignment model | Supplier provides stock for sale, payment after sale | Reduces importer cash burden | Supplier carries risk and may resist |
| Joint marketing arrangement | Supplier and importer jointly develop market | Supports partnership | Requires trust and clear roles |
| Exclusive distribution | Importer gets exclusive market rights | Strong position if demand exists | Dangerous before supplier and demand are proven |

## 39.2 Research View On Long-Term Arrangements

Long-term arrangements should not be entered into before quality, documentation, logistics and South African buyer demand are proven. The research suggests that early arrangements should be flexible, sample-led and non-exclusive.

A long-term arrangement becomes more appropriate once:

- Supplier identity is verified.
- Quality consistency is demonstrated.
- At least one pilot shipment succeeds.
- South African buyers place repeat orders.
- Landed cost is understood.
- Claims and dispute processes are defined.
- Documentation and logistics operate reliably.

## 39.3 Key Contractual Research Points

Any long-term arrangement should research and define:

| Contract Area | Research Requirement |
|---|---|
| Quality specification | Species, grade, process, moisture, defects, cup score |
| Sample matching | Shipment must match approved pre-shipment sample |
| Volume | Minimum and maximum volumes by period |
| Pricing basis | Fixed price, market-linked price, differential or negotiated lot price |
| Incoterms | FOB, CIF, CFR, EXW or other |
| Payment terms | Deposit, balance, documents, credit period |
| Documentation | Certificate of origin, phytosanitary certificate, invoice, packing list |
| Claims | Quality claims, shortage, damage, late shipment |
| Insurance | Cargo risk and responsibility transfer |
| Exclusivity | Avoid or limit until demand is proven |
| Termination | Exit rights if quality or delivery fails |

# 40. Expanded Risk Register And Mitigation Framework

A complete market research paper should identify risks across the full value chain, not only at import stage.

| Risk Category | Specific Risk | Potential Impact | Mitigation / Research Response |
|---|---|---|---|
| Agronomic | Poor seedlings or unsuitable varieties | Lower yield and inconsistent quality | Verify varieties and farm practices |
| Climate | Drought, heat, erratic rainfall | Supply shortages and quality deterioration | Diversify suppliers and origins |
| Pest and disease | Coffee berry disease, leaf rust, pests | Yield loss and quality defects | Assess farm support and disease-control practices |
| Harvesting | Unripe or overripe cherry mixing | Defects and buyer rejection | Require picking and sorting standards |
| Processing | Poor fermentation, drying or washing | Cup defects and mould risk | Inspect processing methods and request QC records |
| Moisture | High or uneven moisture | Mould, claims and shipment rejection | Require moisture data and pre-shipment checks |
| Supplier identity | Supplier is not licensed exporter | Export failure or fraud risk | Verify legal entity and export licence |
| Traceability | Lot identity is unclear | Premium value lost and claims harder | Require lot codes and origin records |
| Documentation | Missing certificate or wrong invoice | Customs delays | Use document checklist before shipment |
| Logistics | Inland delays to port | Missed sailing and quality risk | Build route buffers and use experienced forwarders |
| Port | Congestion or container delays | Increased costs and late delivery | Use realistic timelines and freight alternatives |
| Freight | Freight cost increases | Margin pressure | Quote validity and scenario modelling |
| Insurance | Cargo not insured or underinsured | Financial loss from damage | Marine cargo insurance required |
| Customs | Incorrect HS classification | Delays, penalties or duty errors | Use clearing agent and pre-clearance review |
| VAT | VAT cash flow underestimated | Working-capital stress | Model VAT timing and funding requirement |
| Storage | Poor warehouse conditions | Quality degradation | Use approved warehouse and stock checks |
| Buyer demand | Buyers do not reorder | Stock overhang | Sample-led demand validation before import |
| Price | Global coffee price movements | Margin volatility | Short quote validity and hedging logic where relevant |
| Currency | USD/ZAR volatility | Landed cost changes | FX buffer and fast pricing cycles |
| Payment | Customer pays late | Cash-flow strain | Credit checks and payment terms |
| Reputation | Sustainability claims unsupported | Buyer distrust | Evidence-based ESG claims only |
| Legal | Contract terms unclear | Disputes and losses | Written agreements and claims process |

# 41. Import-To-Sale Control Gates

A practical market research framework should include control gates. These control gates ensure that the opportunity is evaluated objectively before capital is committed.

| Gate | Stage | Minimum Evidence Required | Go / No-Go Logic |
|---:|---|---|---|
| 1 | Supplier identification | Legal identity, role, contact, location | Do not proceed if supplier identity is unclear |
| 2 | Export readiness | Export licence or licensed exporter pathway | Do not proceed without export route |
| 3 | Coffee availability | Coffee type, grade, volume and sample availability | Do not proceed without product clarity |
| 4 | Sample validation | Samples received and evaluated | Do not proceed to shipment without sample approval |
| 5 | Buyer interest | Buyer feedback and pilot interest | Do not import material volume without demand evidence |
| 6 | Landed cost | Supplier price and logistics cost model | Do not proceed if margin is structurally negative |
| 7 | Documentation | Invoice, packing list, origin, phytosanitary where required | Do not ship without document readiness |
| 8 | Logistics | Route, freight, insurance, clearing agent confirmed | Do not ship without logistics accountability |
| 9 | Financial exposure | Deposit, VAT, working capital and payment cycle understood | Do not proceed if funding gap is unresolved |
| 10 | Pilot approval | Internal approval and buyer allocation | Proceed only with controlled pilot |

# 42. Additional Industry Areas To Consider Before Strategy

To complete the research base, the following areas should be included or investigated before finalising the separate strategy document.

## 42.1 Roasting Yield And Weight Loss

Green coffee loses weight during roasting due mainly to moisture and volatile compounds. Typical roasting loss can materially affect the economics of roasted coffee products. If the future strategy includes roasting or private label, the financial model must convert green coffee cost into roasted coffee cost after roast loss.

## 42.2 Shelf Life And Freshness

Green coffee has a longer storage life than roasted coffee, but it still requires appropriate storage. Roasted coffee loses freshness faster and requires stronger packaging discipline. This reinforces why green coffee is the better research-backed starting category.

## 42.3 Quality Claims Process

Any import arrangement should define what happens if shipped coffee does not match the approved sample. A claims process should include retained samples, inspection period, defect thresholds, replacement, discount or refund mechanisms.

## 42.4 Buyer Procurement Behaviour

South African buyers may not switch suppliers only because a new origin is available. Buyers usually require quality, reliability, price consistency, sample approval and confidence that repeat supply can be delivered.

## 42.5 Working Capital And Stock Rotation

The viability of coffee import depends heavily on how quickly stock converts to cash. Slow stock rotation can destroy an apparently attractive margin. Future modelling should include inventory days, customer payment terms, VAT timing and supplier payment terms.

## 42.6 Supplier Development

If suppliers have good coffee but weak export systems, supplier development may be required. This could include documentation support, quality templates, sampling process, lot tracking and planning discipline. Supplier development creates value but also adds effort and risk.

## 42.7 Multi-Origin Diversification

Long-term resilience may require more than one supplier, one origin or one route. Uganda and Ethiopia could be complementary, but future strategy should also monitor Tanzania, Rwanda, Kenya, Burundi, Brazil and Vietnam as benchmark origins.

## 42.8 Market Benchmarking

Uganda and Ethiopia should be compared against Brazil, Tanzania, Rwanda, Kenya, Vietnam, Colombia and Guatemala on price, quality, availability, reliability and buyer appeal.

# 43. Expanded Research Conclusions

The expanded research reinforces the original Version 2.0 conclusion: South Africa represents a real coffee import market, green coffee is the dominant import format, and Uganda and Ethiopia both already participate in South African imports.

However, the additional value-chain analysis shows that viability depends on far more than import statistics. The market opportunity must be assessed across the full chain from farm origination to final buyer sale. A viable long-term import model requires credible suppliers, repeatable quality, disciplined documentation, reliable logistics, realistic landed-cost economics, buyer validation and sustainability evidence.

The research also confirms that Uganda and Ethiopia should be understood differently. Uganda's stronger research position is commercial-scale relevance, Robusta strength, blend economics and potentially lower entry risk. Ethiopia's stronger research position is premium Arabica, specialty recognition, origin storytelling and higher-value buyer segments.

Financial viability is not proven by supplier price alone. It depends on landed cost, VAT cash flow, freight, insurance, storage, buyer price, stock turnover, credit terms and quality-claim exposure. Sustainability is also not a marketing layer; it is part of long-term supply reliability and buyer trust.

This document should therefore be used as the market research foundation for a separate Strategy and Market Entry Report. The strategy document should convert these findings into decisions on pilot design, supplier selection, buyer targeting, operating model, financial model, risk governance and implementation roadmap.
